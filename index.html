Weekly Blog Posts

  • Week 1 (7/22 - 7/26): Come On and Slam, and Welcome to Japan

Elevator Pitch

The goal is to develop an AR platform for art and culture. The app will incorporate 3D spatialized audio so the user can explore an environment and hear music as if it were emanating from the real world around them.

Experience Goal

We want users to engage with art and culture in a way they’ve never experienced before. We want to encourage the users to walk around in the physical world to find new art pieces in the virtual world.

Key Features

  • Location-based AR: We need our application to create displays in a digital environment based on the real-life location of the user. We want these displays to be rendered such that their position remains fixed as the user approaches them. The displays will be specific to different locations in Japan.
  • GPS location recognition: We would like the application to automatically be able to determine which displays will be needed based on the GPS location of the user. This means that based on the specific site the user is visiting, the app will know what information will need to be generated.
  • GPS-triggered audio: As users progress through their exploration of various historical sites, we would like for the app to generate opportunities for the user to hear audio recordings corresponding to their location within the site. These audio recordings could be things like a tour guide, or historical information about a specific nearby location, etc.
  • 3D audio: Additionally, the audio could be played in a 3D fashion so that as the user gets closer to the “source” of the audio in the real world, the audio might change in some way, perhaps by getting louder or making specific sounds more prominent.
  • 360° video: For some occasions, we would like the application to bring up 360° video for the user to experience. This can allow them to better experience an environment that they may not be able to physically explore themselves.
  • 2D AR image display: Similarly, we want the app to display 2D images in augmented reality at specific locations at a site to enrich the site-seeing experience for the user.
  • Building/object recognition: Instead of relying on AR markers in the real world to generate our augmented reality content, we want the app to be able to generate content based on recognizing the buildings and objects around the user in the real world. This way, there is no need to make any alterations to the real world location to accommodate our app, nor any need for the user to carry around an additional peripheral with them as they explore.

Tech Research

There are a couple reasons we are looking into cross-platform solutions that do not use Unity. Unity is a fully featured game engine, which includes a physics engine, robust scene editor, and many other advanced features that we will likely never need. Ultimately, none of us are very familiar with it, so we have taken some time to look at cross-platform solutions that allow us to use tools and languages we are already experienced with. Given the timeframe, we are trying to manage the learning curve.

At the same time, Unity seems to be the most ubiquitous way to build cross-platform AR apps, so using Unity is still a strong possibility; it might be the best decision in the long run.

One of the first alternatives we looked into was ViroReact, which allows you to create cross-platform AR/VR apps using React Native. We were able to get basic AR applications up and running on our phones using the ViroMedia testbed. We will continue to experiment with ViroReact to see if it fully meets our needs. ViroReact supports image recognition, 360 images and video, plane selection and recognition, animated 3D models and more. We can also utilize other React libraries.

We also looked into some web-based solutions, but they all seem to be completely marker-based, which isn’t sufficient for the project. AR.js is one of the only web-based AR projects that is maintained, and it is exclusively marker-based.

We will continue to look for a cross-platform solution that is the best fit for us, and choose one to move forward with as soon as possible.

Roadblocks and Potential Challenges

  • Phones to test on: we have one iPhone 8 and a Galaxy S7. It would be nice to have one or two more test devices.
  • We will need to test the efficacy of using object recognition on large 3D objects such as building facades. These may look very different depending on the lighting and time of day.
  • GPS accuracy may also pose a problem when trying to resolve small differences in position within a historical site.
  • Targeting multiple platforms already limits us to far fewer frameworks than we would otherwise have access to if we were targeting a single platform.

Milestones/Schedule

Total Available Time: 11 weeks
Weeks 1 and 2: learning chosen platforms and how to make AR apps. Also start learning basic object recognition
Weeks 3-6: alpha development

  • Week 3: 360 degree videos, 3D objects, 2D images
  • Week 4: building/object recognition to display material or play audio
  • Week 5: 3D audio
  • Week 6: GPS capabilities
Weeks 7-9: beta development (finalizing features, debugging, testing)
Weeks 10-11: incremental improvements, testing, and bug fixing

Stretch Goals

If we achieve these goals, we were hoping to add some sort of networking feature to the app. This could involve leaving notes in the world for other users to read, or even creating virtual art galleries for other users to download and walk through in AR. We could also try to port the experience into VR using 360 degree videos so you don’t have to physically be at the location to get the experience. These ideas are not fleshed out at this point, but we should be able to evaluate their feasibility as we progress.
